Unpredictable Burger

A physical computing project based on Murakami's story

THE BRIEF

"The Second Bakery Attack" (Japanese: パン屋再襲撃, Hepburn: Pan'ya Saishūgeki) is a short story by Haruki Murakami, originally published in the August 1985 issue of Marie Claire Japan. It is a sequel to Murakami's short story "Bakery Attack", which was published in 1981.
In 1986, "The Second Bakery Attack" was included in a short story collection of the same name. The story was later translated into English by Jay Rubin and published in the January 1992 issue of Playboy. In 1993, Rubin's translation was included in the collection The Elephant Vanishes.
(Wikipedia)

The story is told by the same character from the original “Bakery Attack”, now older and recently married. One night, both he and his wife get unbearably hungry to the point they can not sleep. Unfortunately, they have little food in their refrigerator. Despite eating it all, the urgent hunger would not go away. In that moment, the man has a memory from his past, and he recounts to his wife when he had the exact same feeling with his friend, that time when they decide to robber a bakery, ten years ago.
The two intended to take all the bread, but the man who ran the bakery offers a counterproposal: since he is a Wagener fanatic, if they listen to Tannhäuser and The Flying Dutchman with him in the bakery, he will give them all the bread they needed.

After hearing that story, the woman suggests to do the same thing. However, they can not find an open bakery that late at night, so they decide to robber a McDonald's instead. With ski masks on and an automatic shotgun, they enter the shop and demand thirty Big Macs.
After a discussion with the employees, the couple receive what they asked for, they drive away until they find an empty parking lot and eat until full.
Finally the hunger is gone. From a close reading of Murakami's text we had to choose a particular theme or topic to explore in the creation of an artwork.

PREVIOUS ANALYSIS

Even if at first it may seem just a crazy story, after a preliminary research about the contest in which it was written, the author's ideas regarding the story and the analysis conducted on the text, it is possible to discuss that in reality “The Second Bakery Attack” shows a deep reflection about different themes and symbols.

Hunger

In the first place, the hunger. The wife claims that it is a curse that the husband has brought upon her. It symbolises the need to complete unfinished business: in this case, the idea is that the man has not completed his robbery in the first attack, so the curse was on him to do it. It is also liked to the overwhelming desire for food, that drove the man and his wife to commit a crime and to the symbol of the volcano, representing the curse of the past.

Past and Present

Another theme touched by Murakami is the boundary between the past and the present through the memory of the first bakery attack. The comparison between what was more and exchange rather than a robbery, the difference between the usage of weapons and the feelings that would remain afterwards.
The main character is experiencing a disconnection, a lack of balance between the past and the present and a contrast between his old values and his modern life.
This is symbolised by the boat and the water metaphor.

Gender Roles

The story underlines also the reversion of stereotypes about gender roles: while the man seems very submissive, his wife is in control of the situation. She is insistent of robbing a bakery and when they can not find any, she is the one who suggest to robber a McDoonald's.
She seems prepared to what comes next, in contrast to her husband.

Capitalism

The whole story represent a revolt against consumerism and capitalism, represented by McDonald's. The narrator felt this consumerist culture in the first robbery, when he had to listen to Wagner to earn the bread, and now he express his dislike for this.
Related to the future consumeristic culture that would explode in Japan, the narrator asks to his wife if the robbery was really necessary.
She replies that it was while falling asleep, leaving the man with his thoughts.

Future

In correlation to these themes, “The Second Bakery Attack” underlines also a reflection about the future and the fact that nothing is in our control.
The introduction of the story contains the reflection of the narrator about why he told his wife about the first bakery attack.
Even if it is composed of just a few sentences, it is full of meaning and it also encourages the readers to interrogate themselves.

“I myself have adopted the position that, in fact, we never choose anything at all. Things happen. Or not.”

THE CONCEPT

I focused on two details of the story:
▪ The sentence “we never choose anything at all. Things happen. Or not.” for the theoretical aspect
▪ The McDonald's Burger for the practical aspect

Following a research, I found out that the McDonald's employees have to follow a designed structure and protocol to produce their burgers.
In this case, in fact, the employees movements in the shop and their actions on the burgers are quite predictable.
However, Murakami's story underline how the right actions may produce wrong results and vice versa, because things aren't always what they seem, but they might change or they might mean something else.
So, I was questioning myself: what if the burger is not a real burger?
The story is composed by metaphors, so what if the burger would represent the user's life and each layer would be an experience that might occur?
Related to this reasoning, the final project consists in a physical burger and a virtual one, displayed on a computer screen.
The user has all the ingredients in front of him (the crown and the heel of the burger, the hamburger, the cheese, the onions and the pickles) and his goal is to assembly them in the right way, understanding the connection between the physical object and the one displayed.
That said, because as soon as he begin to stack one layer on to the other, he will realise that the physical thing does not correspond to the virtual one (e.g. the physical cheese may correspond to the displayed onions).
Only one combination of ingredients is correct, and every time that he fails, he is “reminded” of his helplessness in front of unexpected events with some sentences extrapolated from the story and displayed randomly.
